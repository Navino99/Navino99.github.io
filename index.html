Deep GRC Knowledge

Structured understanding of governance, risk, compliance, and financial crime frameworks built through disciplined study and professional practice.

🏛️ Governance & Principled Performance
OCEG · Three Lines · Board Accountability ▼

Governance defines how an organization is directed, controlled, and held accountable. At the foundation is the OCEG concept of Principled Performance — reliably achieving objectives, addressing uncertainty, and acting with integrity simultaneously. The Board sets strategy and risk appetite; senior management translates it into operational programs; the Three Lines of Defense model distributes accountability across business operations, risk/compliance functions, and internal audit.

OCEG Red Book COAPDRIM Three Lines Board Oversight Risk Appetite Accountability
📊 Risk Management Lifecycle
ISO 31000 · Risk Matrix · Treatment Strategies ▼

Risk management follows a structured four-step lifecycle: Identify → Assess → Treat → Monitor. Risk is calculated as Likelihood × Impact, with treatment strategies of Mitigate, Avoid, Transfer, or Accept. Residual risk after controls must fall within the organization's defined risk tolerance. Business continuity planning defines RTO (Recovery Time Objective) and RPO (Recovery Point Objective) to bound acceptable downtime and data loss.

Risk Register Risk Matrix RTO / RPO Mitigate Transfer Accept ISO 31000
🏦 Financial Crime Compliance (FCC)
AML · KYC · Sanctions · Fraud ▼

Financial Crime Compliance covers AML (money laundering through Placement → Layering → Integration), Counter-Terrorism Financing, sanctions screening against OFAC/UN/EU lists, Anti-Bribery & Corruption (FCPA, UK Bribery Act), and fraud prevention. Core controls include KYC/CDD/EDD, transaction monitoring systems, PEP screening, and Suspicious Activity Reporting (SAR/STR). The ING Bank €775M fine (2018) demonstrates the catastrophic cost of AML compliance failure.

AML KYC/CDD/EDD OFAC Sanctions SAR/STR PEP Screening FCPA UK Bribery Act
🔐 Security Controls & Secure Design
Preventive · Detective · Corrective · Defense in Depth ▼

Security controls are classified by purpose (Preventive / Detective / Corrective) and by nature (Administrative / Technical / Physical). Defense in Depth requires multiple overlapping control layers — no single point of failure. Secure design principles including Least Privilege, Domain Separation, Process Isolation, Minimization, and Information Hiding reduce the attack surface at the architecture level — before tools are even deployed.

Least Privilege Defense in Depth Zero Trust MFA SIEM Encryption Secure SDLC
🔗 Vendor & Third-Party Risk
Due Diligence · SLA · DPA · Continuous Monitoring ▼

Third-party relationships are one of the most significant and underestimated risk vectors for organizations. The SolarWinds supply chain attack (2020) demonstrated that vendor compromise can be more impactful than direct attacks. Vendor risk management requires a lifecycle approach: tiered due diligence, contractual security obligations (DPA, NDA, right-to-audit, breach notification), continuous monitoring, and defined offboarding procedures.

Due Diligence Risk Tiering DPA SLA Right to Audit Supply Chain Risk
real-world analysis

GRC Case Studies

Analysing major compliance failures to extract practical lessons for risk and governance professionals.

AML · COMPLIANCE FAILURE · 2018
ING Bank — €775M Fine
ING Bank failed to prevent its accounts from being used for money laundering by criminal organizations over several years. Failures included inadequate KYC, poorly calibrated transaction monitoring, and a compliance culture subordinate to commercial objectives.
Lesson: Compliance functions must be independent, adequately resourced, and genuinely empowered to block business when necessary. A compliance culture that prioritizes revenue over integrity will eventually produce catastrophic regulatory consequences.
GOVERNANCE FAILURE · CULTURE · 2016
Wells Fargo — $3B in Fines
Employees opened 3.5 million unauthorized accounts due to an aggressive sales culture with perverse incentives. The Board failed to challenge the culture; internal audit failed to escalate the systemic pattern; whistleblowers were suppressed.
Lesson: Governance without ethical culture is ineffective. Incentive structures can overpower compliance rules. Whistleblower protection is essential for any effective compliance program.
SUPPLY CHAIN · CYBER · 2020
SolarWinds — Nation-State Attack
Attackers injected malicious code into SolarWinds' build pipeline — distributed to 18,000+ organizations including US government agencies. The breach remained undetected for nine months. ISO 27001:2022 added new supply chain controls in direct response.
Lesson: Supply chain risk requires the same rigor as direct vendor risk. Software integrity verification (SBOM, code signing) and Zero Trust architecture are essential countermeasures.
GDPR · DATA BREACH · 2020
British Airways — £20M ICO Fine
Attackers injected card-skimming code into the BA website, harvesting 400,000 customers' payment data. The breach was not detected by BA's own systems — discovered by a third-party researcher. Fined under GDPR Article 32 for inadequate technical controls.
Lesson: GDPR Article 32 requires security appropriate to the risk — not minimum controls. Web application security testing must be conducted regularly. Detection capabilities must extend to public-facing systems.
